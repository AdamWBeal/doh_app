Welcome to the NYC Department of Health Restaurant Inspection Primer

The purpose of this website is to aid NYC restaurant's in navigating the city's Department of Health inspection process. Type in any food service establishment to see relevant information to keep your customers safe and your bills low. Thanks for stopping by.

Why did you make this?

I conceived the original seed for this project while managing a busy bakery café in Tribeca, Manhattan. In addition to balancing extortionist rent, constantly shifting staff and the million other things that go into managing a restaurant there was always the spectre of the surprise health inspection from the NYC Department of Health and Mental Hygiene, whose fines can potentially reach into the thousands of dollars. If the inspections were inevitable, how could I use the open source data on provided by the city (https://bit.ly/3I8zsqO) to give me any advantage? Skip ahead a few years and I pursued the same question as my capstone project for General Assembly’s data science immersive program. This web app is the result of this effort. The intended end user here is an NYC restaurant stakeholder who wishes to have just a little bit more information to help them navigate the inspection process.

What is this web app trying to tell me?

The line chart represents the conditional probability of “surviving” until at least until the given time. Put another way, at that same given time only X% of the population has not been inspected yet. Using this you can (hopefully) make a more informed plan for your inspection. The next segment shows the all the selected restaurant’s prior (and upheld) violations. This can serve as a refresher on which issues to address first when an inspection is on the horizon. There is no need to repeat the same mistakes. The final section gives the user a baseline of their particular time and place. It shows the top 15 violations within roughly a half mile radius and occurring in the current month.

A word on COVID

From March 17, 2020 to July 23, 2021 the DOH suspended random inspections. This has profound implications regarding the ability of the model to forecast with confidence. In fact, less than 20% of New York restaurants (as of November 2021) have had a “post-covid” inspection. Due to the nature of the underlying model and its assumptions the timing prediction will not prove particularly useful in the short to medium term. What also remains to be seen is how and whether the DOHMH is able to simultaneously catch up with the covid backlog as well as fulfil their mandate to conduct regular inspections according to DOH policy. In any regard, assuming the DOH doesn’t change their currently open source reporting method over the next couple of years the model should regain some predictive value, and if your restaurant in question has had a recent inspection the model will hopefully reflect a return to normal policies and timings. Furthermore, I will from time to time revisit the assumptions made regarding manipulation of the raw data and model assumptions to see if there’s a better way to account for the disruption COVID caused.
